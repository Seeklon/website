# Remote vs In-Office: Adapting Your Recruitment Strategy in 2026

In 2026, the question is no longer really "should we offer remote work?" — most candidates for office and tech roles now expect it as a baseline. The real question is: **how do you adapt your recruitment process to match the working model you're offering?**

Hiring for a fully remote role, a 3/2 hybrid arrangement, or a fully on-site position aren't the same process. The interview questions differ. The channels to prioritise aren't the same. And onboarding must be designed differently.

Here's a practical guide to adapting your strategy to each configuration.

---

## What Candidates Expect in 2026

The market has shifted profoundly since 2020. Here's what the data shows:

- **58% of UK workers** say they would turn down a role with no remote working option (Glassdoor, 2025)
- Job ads mentioning remote work receive on average **50% more applications** (LinkedIn Talent Insights, 2025)
- Hybrid working (2–3 remote days) is now the preference of **54% of employees** who have experienced remote work
- Only **18% of professionals** want to return to fully in-office working

These figures don't mean in-office work has disappeared — but they mean you need to justify and communicate your proposition clearly, whatever it is.

---

## How to Write Your Job Ad by Working Model

### For a Fully In-Office Role

Transparency is essential. Don't just slip "on-site" into the small print. Explain why, and highlight what it brings.

**What to include:**
- A clear operational reason (direct client contact, equipment handling, essential real-time collaboration...)
- Compensating advantages (strong team culture, attractive offices, benefits, flexible hours...)
- Exact address and commuting facilities (transport links, parking, on-site catering)

**What to avoid:**
- ❌ Writing "office-based, no exceptions" without explanation
- ❌ Implying remote might be possible if it isn't
- ❌ Neglecting the working environment in the description

### For a Hybrid Role

Be specific about the arrangement. "Remote flexibility" means nothing. Candidates want to know:

- How many days per week remote? (fixed or flexible?)
- Are those days chosen by the employee or assigned by the manager?
- Are there mandatory in-office days (team meetings, etc.)?
- Does the policy apply from day one or after the probationary period?

**Example of effective wording**: "This role is hybrid: 3 days in the office (including Tuesdays as a full-team day) and 2 days remote at your discretion, from the end of the probationary period."

### For a Fully Remote Role

Clearly state whether the candidate can be based anywhere in the country, or whether there are geographic constraints. Also specify:

- Expected common working hours
- Frequency of in-person meetings (if applicable)
- Equipment provided (laptop, monitor, internet access?)
- Policy for reimbursing home office expenses

## Assessing a Candidate Remotely: What Changes

A video interview is not an in-person interview filmed. The dynamics are different, and the assessment must adapt.

### What You Perceive Less Well on Video

- Full body language (you only see the upper body)
- Room energy and group dynamics
- The candidate's actual working environment (indirect signals about organisation)

### What You Must Add for Remote Roles

If you're hiring for a remote position, specifically assess the competencies that predict success working independently:

**1. Autonomy and self-organisation**
> "Describe a typical remote working week for you. How do you prioritise without the natural interactions of an office?"

**2. Asynchronous communication**
> "How do you handle a situation where you're waiting on a blocking piece of information and your contact is unavailable?"

**3. Discipline and focus**
> "What have you put in place to protect your concentration when working from home?"

**4. Proactive communication**
> "Give me an example of a situation where you flagged a problem to your team before being asked to."

### Video Quality as a Signal

A remote candidate with an unstable connection, a chaotic background or poor audio gives you information about their actual working environment. It's not automatically disqualifying — but it's worth addressing directly.

## Essential Tools by Working Model

### For Remote/Hybrid Hiring

Your assessment stack should include:

- **Video conferencing tool**: Zoom, Teams or Google Meet (avoid untested platforms)
- **Collaboration platform** to share documents and evaluation rubrics (Notion, Google Drive)
- **Centralised ATS** to manage applications regardless of geographic origin
- **Remote testing tools** if you use aptitude assessments (ensure GDPR compliance and validity)

### For a Standard In-Person Process

- **Dedicated, quiet interview room** (nothing undermines an interview like noise or interruptions)
- **Smooth reception logistics** (welcome, refreshments, punctuality)
- **ATS for tracking** even when interviews are in person

## Remote Onboarding: Keys to Success

Remote onboarding is one of the most delicate stages. According to BambooHR, 30% of employees leave within the first 90 days — a risk even higher when onboarding is poorly designed for remote.

### Mistakes to Avoid

- ❌ Sending equipment on the start date (too late)
- ❌ Leaving the new hire to find their way around internal documentation alone
- ❌ Planning no informal interactions (virtual coffees work — if they're regular)
- ❌ Forgetting to introduce the wider team beyond the direct manager

### An Effective Remote Onboarding Plan

**Before Day 1:**
- Equipment delivered and configured (laptop, access credentials, passwords)
- Invited to all communication tools (Slack, Teams, etc.)
- Warm welcome email with the first week's programme

**Week 1:**
- Welcome call with the manager (Day 1 morning)
- Individual introduction to each team member (15 minutes each)
- Training on internal tools and platforms
- Virtual team lunch (yes, it does build connection)

**Weeks 2 to 4:**
- Short daily check-ins (15 minutes) with the manager
- First assignments with prompt feedback
- Access to internal documentation and knowledge base

**Months 2 and 3:**
- Structured weekly one-to-ones
- 90-day objectives set and agreed from the start
- An in-person session if possible (team meeting, office visit)

## Legal Basics: What You Need to Know About Remote Work

### Formalising the Arrangement

If you're offering regular remote working, it should be formalised in writing — in the employment contract, an addendum, or a flexible working policy. This protects both parties.

The arrangement should specify at minimum:
- Which days are remote
- The expected working hours and availability windows
- How and when the employee should return to the office
- How home office costs are handled

### Reimbursing Home Working Costs

Employers are expected to cover additional costs directly attributable to working from home. In practice, this often takes the form of a monthly home working allowance (HMRC allows up to £6 per week tax-free without receipts, or higher amounts with evidence).

### The Right to Disconnect

All employees — including remote workers — retain the right not to be contacted outside working hours. This is increasingly treated as a health and safety matter, not just a courtesy. Set clear expectations in your remote working policy.

### Flexible Working Requests

As of 2024, employees in the UK have the right to request flexible working from day one of employment. While you can decline on legitimate business grounds, you must respond within two months and provide a clear reason. Build a simple process for handling these requests.

## Conclusion: The Right Strategy Depends on Your Reality

There's no universally right or wrong answer on what working model to offer. It depends on your operational constraints, your culture, and your talent market.

What matters is being clear, consistent, and delivering what you promise. A candidate who discovers after joining that the "2 days remote" they were promised doesn't exist in practice will become a disengaged employee — and a negative ambassador for your employer brand.

> Flexibility is a competitive advantage. But only if it's genuine and clearly communicated.
